# AI PM Substack Writing Style
# Reference: Aakash Gupta (@aakashgupta) - Practical AI Agents for PMs

Use this when writing Substack posts, LinkedIn articles, or long-form AI PM content.

## Characteristics

**Tone:** Authoritative, conversational, direct
**Audience:** Product managers who want to stay ahead of AI
**Goal:** Give practitioners a clear framework they can use immediately

---

## Opening Hook

Always start with a single provocative line that creates tension or stakes.

- "Your product's next million users won't have eyes."
- "AI product building is a rich man and a poor man's game."
- "Zapier now rates PMs on their AI usage."

Follow with 2-3 short lines that expand the tension. Then a rhetorical question. Then: *That's what you'll learn today.*

## Structure

**1. Setup block**
- 1-sentence hook
- 2-3 lines expanding the problem
- Rhetorical question ("So how do you get that 'transformative' rating?")
- Italic line: *That's what you'll learn today.*

**2. "Today's Post" section**
- Italic intro: *I cover the X key [things]:*
- Numbered list previewing all sections
- Note which sections are free vs. paid (if applicable)

**3. Numbered sections**
- Bold H2: `1. Section Title - short punchy subtitle`
- 2-4 short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each)
- 1 custom infographic or diagram per major section
- End section with a bold callout or key takeaway

**4. Credibility anchor**
- Weave in personal experience naturally: "I've seen this at every company I've worked at."
- Embed expert tweets or quotes as visual blocks
- Reference real companies by name (Stripe, Cloudflare, Shopify — never "major companies")

---

## Voice Rules

### Do
- Short sentences. Fragments for emphasis.
- Rhetorical questions that pull the reader forward
- **Bold** for the key insight in each section
- *Italic* for dramatic beats and personal asides
- Specific numbers always: "97M MCP downloads", "$1.4M per year fully loaded"
- Name real people: "Andrej Karpathy put it bluntly: 'It's 2026. Build. For. Agents.'"
- Personal credibility: "I gave this as a talk at Berkeley Product Con"
- Contractions everywhere: don't, it's, you're, we're

### Don't
- No em dashes
- No "leverage," "utilize," "unlock," "streamline," "robust," "cutting-edge"
- No paragraphs longer than 3 sentences
- No generic examples ("some companies") — always use real names
- No soft hedging ("perhaps," "might consider")
- No AI-sounding openers ("In today's rapidly evolving landscape...")

---

## Depth Standards

Every major claim needs one of:
- A specific number or stat ("9 out of 10 engineers cannot articulate their product's strategy")
- A real company example (Stripe, GitHub, Zapier)
- An expert quote embedded as a tweet screenshot or blockquote
- A named framework (7 PM AI Skills, 5 Distribution Channels, 4-step sizing framework)

Frameworks should be:
- Named and defined clearly
- Illustrated with a custom diagram/infographic
- Applied with a concrete example

---

## Visuals

Each major section should have one of:
- A framework diagram (e.g., "The 5 Distribution Channels" map)
- A comparison table (e.g., Zapier's Unacceptable → Transformative rubric)
- A bold callout graphic with key stat or takeaway
- An embedded tweet from a credible source

Callout graphics follow this pattern:
```
[Bold headline — the key insight]
[1-2 supporting lines with data]
Takeaway: [Memorable one-liner in large text]
```

## Tone Calibration Examples

**Too generic:**
> AI is changing how product managers work. There are many tools available.

**Right:**
> Some PMs barely even use ChatGPT. Others are running 40-person AI agent teams in Claude Code. The gap between an 'unacceptable' and a 'transformative' rating is bigger than ever.

---

**Too corporate:**
> Organizations should consider leveraging AI capabilities to streamline their product development workflows.

**Right:**
> Use AI as a thinking partner, not a replacement. It can stress-test your assumptions. It can't replace the judgment you build from sitting with your CEO every week.
